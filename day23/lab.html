Consumer Lag Incident  ·  booking.confirmed topic  ·  4.1M events unprocessed

The letters are piling up. Nobody is collecting them.

Saturday 09:00 · Email service consumer group · Lag growing at 12,000/min

Saturday morning. A Grafana alert fires: consumer lag on the booking.confirmed topic, email-service group, has crossed 500,000. By the time the on-call engineer checks the dashboard, it is at 1.2 million and climbing at 12,000 events per minute.

Bookings are still being processed — the checkout API is fine. Payments are going through. But no confirmation emails have been sent since Friday 23:15. Users are booking tickets and hearing nothing. Support tickets are flooding in.

The sorting centre is receiving letters. The bins are filling. The delivery van stopped collecting 10 hours ago.

Step 4: Recovery plan

Recovery
The producer has been fixed and is back to JSON format from offset 1,465,000 onward. How do you recover the 4.7M unprocessed events?
What this incident teaches
▶Schema Registry would have caught this at deploy time. If the producer had registered its schema and the new pipe-delimited format violated backward compatibility rules, the deploy would have been blocked. The consumer never would have seen an invalid event. This is why Schema Registry is on the checklist — not for convenience, but for exactly this scenario.
▶Consumer lag by partition reveals which consumer is stuck. The output showed partitions 0 and 1 at zero lag, partitions 2-5 at 1.18M each. This is the fingerprint of a consumer that stalled on a specific partition — not a throughput problem. A throughput problem would show growing lag across all partitions evenly.
▶Kafka retained the bad events — replay was possible. Because Kafka had 7-day retention and the incident was caught within 10 hours, every unparseable event was still in the topic. The schema-tolerant consumer could replay from offset 281448 and process all 4.7M events. With a message queue (SQS, RabbitMQ), those events would have been lost when the consumer failed to ack.
▶Alert on growing lag, not absolute lag value. The alert fired at 500,000. But the lag had been growing since 23:15 — 10 hours earlier. An alert that fires when lag growth rate exceeds 1,000/min would have caught this within 10 minutes of the bad deploy. Absolute lag thresholds are a lagging indicator. Growth rate is the leading one.
