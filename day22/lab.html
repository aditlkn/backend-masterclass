Step 4: What actually happened

What this scenario teaches
▶Error budgets don't make decisions — they inform them. The budget at 3% doesn't automatically mean "don't ship." It means the cost of shipping is now much higher than normal, and someone with authority needs to own that decision explicitly. The SRE's job is to surface the data, not to block the business unilaterally.
▶Expressing risk as expected budget cost is more persuasive than "it might fail." "20% chance of a 22-minute rollback = 4.4 expected minutes consumed, we have 1.3 minutes left, so this deploy has >100% expected budget cost" is a precise statement that a VP can respond to rationally. "It might break things" is easy to dismiss.
▶The canary approach is often the right answer. A 30-minute canary at 5% traffic exposes the deploy to real production conditions while limiting blast radius to 5% of users. If the canary is clean, you've significantly reduced the risk of a full rollout. If it fires an error budget alarm in the first 10 minutes, you rollback before consuming more than a few minutes of budget.
▶The budget policy needs to be agreed before the crisis. The reason this conversation is hard is that the policy wasn't written down. If engineering and product had agreed in advance: "below 10% budget, all non-critical deploys require VP sign-off with explicit risk acknowledgment" — this would be a 2-minute Slack thread, not a tense Thursday morning negotiation.
