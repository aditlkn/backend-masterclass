Section 01

The flight delay board — and how airlines manage reliability

You're at the airport. You look up at the departure board. Your flight shows: On Time. You relax. You know what "on time" means — the plane leaves within 15 minutes of the scheduled time. Not within 2 minutes. Not within an hour. 15 minutes. That's the standard, and it's stated explicitly so everyone — airline, ground crew, passengers — knows whether the flight is meeting it or not.

Now imagine the airline tracked this across all their flights. At the end of the month, 94.2% of flights departed within 15 minutes of schedule. That number is their SLI — Service Level Indicator. It's the measurement of actual performance against a defined standard.

Before the month started, the airline committed to their partners: "95% of our flights will be on time." That's their SLO — Service Level Objective. The promise they made to themselves about the level of reliability they'd achieve.

At 94.2%, they've missed their SLO. The gap — 0.8 percentage points of flights that should have been on time but weren't — is their error budget. They started the month with permission to have 5% of flights be late. They used 5.8% worth. They overspent.

Now the important part. An airline tracking on-time performance isn't doing it to feel bad about delays. They're doing it to make decisions. When they have budget to spare — when they're running at 97% and only need 95% — they can take risks. They can try a new boarding process, experiment with tighter connection windows, run maintenance during peak hours. When the budget is exhausted, they stop the experiments and focus on recovery. The error budget tells you how much risk you're allowed to take right now.

✈️
Where the analogy breaks

Airlines measure on-time departure. Software SLIs are more nuanced — you typically measure success rate (percentage of requests that return a non-5xx response), latency (percentage of requests that complete within a threshold), and availability (percentage of time the service responds at all). These can diverge: a service that's technically "up" but returning 503s on 20% of requests is failing its success-rate SLI even though its availability SLI looks fine. The airline departure board shows one number. Your dashboard needs several.

Why this framing matters

Without SLOs, reliability discussions are vague and political. "We need more reliability work" vs "we need to ship the new feature" is a values argument that never resolves. With SLOs, it becomes factual. You have 2.3 hours of downtime budget left this quarter. The proposed deploy has a 30% chance of causing a 45-minute outage based on historical data. Do you spend 13.5 expected minutes of your remaining 138 minutes on this deploy? Now it's a risk calculation, not a values argument.

Google's SRE book introduced this framing. The insight was that 100% reliability is the wrong target — not because you don't care about users, but because the cost of the last few nines of reliability vastly exceeds the cost of the nines before them, and users genuinely cannot tell the difference between 99.99% and 99.999%. The remaining budget is better spent on shipping features that users can actually notice.

Section 02

SLIs — choosing what to measure

An SLI is a carefully chosen quantitative measure of some aspect of the level of service being provided. The key word is carefully — most services have dozens of things you could measure, but only a few that actually reflect what users experience. Measuring the wrong thing is worse than not measuring at all, because it gives you false confidence.

The four categories

Availability: Is the service responding at all? Usually expressed as a ratio of successful probes to total probes. Simple, but can mask problems — a service returning 503 on 20% of requests may still show 100% availability if your probe just checks for any HTTP response.

Latency: How long do requests take? The critical decision here is which percentile. Average latency hides the worst user experiences. P99 latency tells you what 1 in 100 users experiences. P99.9 tells you what 1 in 1,000 experiences. For a booking system processing 10,000 requests per day, P99.9 represents 10 users per day having the worst possible experience. For a system processing 10,000 requests per second, it's 10 users per second. The same percentile means very different things at different scales.

Success rate (error rate): What fraction of requests complete successfully? "Successfully" needs to be defined — for an API, it typically means 2xx responses. But for a payment service, a 200 response with {"status": "declined"} is a successful API call and a failed business transaction. Know which one your SLI measures.

Throughput: Can the service handle the request volume? Usually a secondary SLI — low throughput causes latency and error rate SLIs to degrade, so measuring those is often sufficient. Throughput SLIs matter when you have quota-based services or batch pipelines where volume is the primary contract.

Good SLIs and bad SLIs

A good SLI measures what the user experiences directly. A bad SLI measures system internals that correlate with user experience but don't represent it. CPU utilisation at 80% is a bad SLI — it tells you the server is busy, but not whether users are suffering. Requests completing within 500ms is a good SLI — it directly measures whether users are waiting too long.

⚠️
The SLI measurement window matters as much as the threshold

A 99% success rate SLI measured over a rolling 28-day window is very different from the same SLI measured over a rolling 1-hour window. The 28-day window smooths over short incidents and represents the user's long-term experience. The 1-hour window catches incidents quickly but produces noisy alerts on normal statistical variation. Most teams need both: the 28-day window for the official SLO, and shorter windows for burn-rate alerting. Section 4 covers this in detail.

Section 03

SLOs — setting the right target

An SLO is the target value for an SLI over a measurement window. "99% of requests succeed over a rolling 28-day window." That's a complete SLO. It has a metric (request success rate), a threshold (99%), a window (28 days), and a measurement approach (rolling — not calendar month).

The most important SLO decision: what percentage?

The right SLO is not the highest number you can achieve. It's the number that correctly reflects what users need — and no more. A payment gateway probably needs 99.95% availability. A developer documentation site probably needs 99%. Setting both at 99.99% would mean spending the same engineering effort on the docs site as on payments, which doesn't make sense.

Start from the user's perspective. How much downtime is acceptable per month? One hour? Four hours? Nine minutes? Convert that to a percentage. Then look at your historical data — what have you actually been achieving? Your initial SLO should be slightly below your historical performance, not aspirational. You can tighten it over time as you understand what's actually possible.

Rolling window vs calendar month

A rolling 28-day window is almost always better than a calendar month. A calendar month SLO resets on the 1st — which means teams burn down their entire error budget on January 2nd and then coast for the rest of the month with nothing to manage. A rolling window means the budget is always "right now, what does our reliability look like in the last 28 days?" It creates continuous accountability rather than a monthly sprint-and-coast cycle.

Multiple SLOs for the same service

Most production services need at least two SLOs: one for availability/success rate and one for latency. These can fail independently — a service can be available (returning responses) while failing its latency SLO (every response takes 10 seconds). Having both catches both failure modes. A third SLO for data freshness or queue drain time makes sense for pipeline services. Beyond three, you're probably over-instrumenting unless your service has genuinely distinct user-facing dimensions.

Section 04

Error budgets — the number that ends the argument

Your error budget is the amount of unreliability you're allowed to have before you've broken your SLO promise. If your SLO is 99.9% success rate over 28 days, and you serve 10 million requests in that window, your budget is 10,000 failed requests. That's it. Every 500 error, every timeout, every failed payment burns from that budget.

The reason error budgets matter isn't the accounting — it's what the budget gates. When you have budget remaining, the implicit agreement is: engineering can take risks. You can ship on Fridays. You can run experiments. You can try a new caching layer. The budget absorbs the inevitable failures that come with change. When the budget is exhausted, the implicit agreement flips: no new features until reliability is restored. The team's focus shifts from building to stabilising.

This is what makes the argument stop. Product can't push for a risky deploy when engineering can show "our error budget is at 4% remaining, this deploy has historically caused 2-hour incidents, and we have 138 minutes of budget left this quarter." That's a factual statement, not a values argument. Product can still decide to take the risk — but they own that decision explicitly rather than pressuring engineering to ignore reliability concerns.

Section 05

Burn rate alerts — detecting problems before budget runs out

The error budget by itself is a lagging indicator — you find out you've exhausted it after it's gone. Burn rate alerts are the early warning system. They tell you: at the current rate of failure, how long until the budget is gone?

A burn rate of 1× means you're consuming budget at exactly the expected rate — you'll use it all up precisely at the end of the window, which means you're right at your SLO. A burn rate of 2× means you're consuming it twice as fast — you'll exhaust it halfway through the window. A burn rate of 14.4× means you'll exhaust the entire monthly budget in 2 hours.

The multi-window alert strategy

Google's SRE workbook recommends alerting at multiple windows simultaneously. A single alert based on a short window (like 5 minutes) fires too often on normal statistical noise. A single alert based on a long window (like 7 days) takes too long to notice fast-burning incidents. The solution is two alerts that must fire together:

  • Fast burn (short + long window): Alert when burn rate > 14.4× for both the last 1 hour AND the last 5 minutes. This catches major incidents quickly — at 14.4×, you exhaust a 28-day budget in 48 hours. Fires fast, high precision.
  • Slow burn (medium windows): Alert when burn rate > 6× for both the last 6 hours AND the last 30 minutes. This catches sustained moderate failures that would exhaust the budget in 5 days without being noticed.

Requiring both windows to fire simultaneously reduces alert noise dramatically. A 5-minute spike that doesn't persist over 1 hour won't page you. A 1-hour elevated error rate that already appeared in the 5-minute window will.

Burn rate alert — Prometheus + GrafanaYAML
# SLO: 99.9% success rate over 28 days
# Error budget: 0.1% = 28 * 24 * 60 * 0.001 = 40.32 minutes of total failure

groups:
  - name: slo_alerts
    rules:
      # Fast burn — major incident, exhaust budget in <48h
      - alert: ErrorBudgetBurnFast
        expr: |
          (
            rate(http_requests_total{status=~"5.."}[1h])
            / rate(http_requests_total[1h])
          ) > (14.4 * 0.001)   # 14.4x burn rate × error budget
          AND
          (
            rate(http_requests_total{status=~"5.."}[5m])
            / rate(http_requests_total[5m])
          ) > (14.4 * 0.001)
        for: 2m
        labels:
          severity: critical
        annotations:
          summary: "Fast error budget burn: exhausts 28d budget in <48h"
          runbook: "https://runbooks.internal/slo-fast-burn"

      # Slow burn — sustained failure, exhaust budget in <5 days
      - alert: ErrorBudgetBurnSlow
        expr: |
          (
            rate(http_requests_total{status=~"5.."}[6h])
            / rate(http_requests_total[6h])
          ) > (6 * 0.001)
          AND
          (
            rate(http_requests_total{status=~"5.."}[30m])
            / rate(http_requests_total[30m])
          ) > (6 * 0.001)
        for: 15m
        labels:
          severity: warning
        annotations:
          summary: "Slow error budget burn: exhausts 28d budget in <5 days"

Section 06

Error budgets in practice — what the team actually does with them

Having an error budget is not the same as using it. The budget only changes team behaviour if it gates actual decisions. Here's how it works in practice at teams that use SLOs effectively.

When budget is healthy (>50% remaining)

The team is free to take risks. Deploys on Fridays are acceptable. Experiments in production (A/B tests, canary releases with broader traffic) are encouraged. Technical debt that requires downtime to address can be scheduled. The implicit message: you've been reliable enough that you've earned the right to move fast.

When budget is getting low (10-50% remaining)

The team increases caution. Deploys get more conservative — smaller changes, more canary time, feature flags for rollback. Planned maintenance gets postponed if it would consume significant budget. On-call rotations get briefed on the remaining budget so they have context for incident decisions. No new large infrastructure changes until the budget recovers.

When budget is exhausted (<10% remaining or burnt through)

This is the forcing function. The team formally freezes feature work and focuses exclusively on reliability. Product and engineering leadership are informed — the SLO violation is a shared problem, not an engineering problem. A post-mortem process kicks off for any incidents that contributed to the burn. The focus shifts to: what caused budget burn this window, and what systemic changes prevent it next window?

✅
The error budget is a conversation tool, not a punishment

The framing matters enormously. "Engineering failed to meet the SLO" is blame. "We burned through our error budget and now we need to pause feature work" is shared accountability. The budget belongs to the team — product and engineering together — not to engineering alone. When a product-driven decision causes an incident that burns budget, product owns that burn alongside engineering. This is what actually changes the dynamic: reliability becomes everyone's problem, not just the on-call engineer's.

Section 07

Common SLO mistakes — and why each one breaks the system

Setting aspirational SLOs instead of historical ones

If your service has historically run at 99.5% availability, setting an SLO of 99.9% doesn't improve reliability — it just means you're always in "budget exhausted" mode. Aspirational SLOs that are regularly violated train teams to ignore them. Start from what you actually achieve, set the SLO just below that, and tighten it incrementally.

Measuring the wrong thing

SLIs that measure infrastructure metrics (CPU, memory, disk I/O) instead of user-facing outcomes (request success rate, latency) give false confidence. A service can have perfect infrastructure metrics while users are experiencing timeouts. Always measure from the user's perspective — the closer to the user, the better.

Not including latency SLOs

Success rate SLOs alone miss an entire class of user suffering. A service that's "up" but taking 8 seconds per request is failing users even if every request eventually succeeds. You need both: "99% of requests succeed" and "95% of requests complete within 500ms."

Treating SLOs as a once-a-quarter activity

SLOs only change team behaviour if they're visible continuously, not just at quarterly reviews. The error budget needs to be on the team's dashboard, visible to everyone, updated in near-real-time. A budget you only check in retrospective is a postmortem tool, not a decision-making tool.

Setting the same SLO for everything

Not every service deserves a 99.9% SLO. Internal admin tools might be fine at 99%. A read replica for non-critical reporting might be fine at 98%. Applying the same strict SLO to every service means spending the same engineering effort on everything — which means underinvesting in the critical services that need it most.

Section 08

Production reality

Start with one SLO on one service

Teams that try to define SLOs for every service simultaneously end up in a six-month process that produces a spreadsheet nobody uses. Start with the most critical user-facing service. Define one SLO. Build the dashboard. Use it for one quarter. Then expand. The first SLO teaches you how to define good SLOs — things that seemed obvious in planning (like what counts as a "successful" request) turn out to be nuanced in practice.

The Prometheus + Grafana stack for SLOs

The standard implementation uses Prometheus for metrics collection, recording rules for efficient SLI calculation over multiple time windows, and Grafana for the dashboard and alert evaluation. The deep dive covers the full setup. The key implementation decision: use Prometheus recording rules to pre-aggregate your SLI metrics, rather than computing them at query time. At high cardinality, real-time computation of error rates over 28-day windows will kill your Prometheus instance.

Sloth: SLO configuration as code

Sloth is an open-source tool that generates Prometheus recording rules and alerts from a simple SLO definition YAML. Instead of manually writing complex multi-window Prometheus rules, you define your SLO once and Sloth generates everything. Worth knowing about before you write burn-rate alert expressions by hand.

External vs internal measurement

Internal metrics (from your Prometheus in the same cluster as your service) can miss failures that affect the network path between users and your service. A load balancer failure might mean users can't reach your service at all — your internal metrics show 0 requests (and thus 0 errors), making everything look fine. Synthetic monitoring — external probes from multiple regions hitting your service like a real user would — catches this class of failure. For critical services, you want both.

Section 09

The things nobody tells you

Your first SLO will be wrong — and that's fine

Every team that implements SLOs discovers that their first SLO is either too strict (they're always in violation, so the SLO gets ignored) or too loose (they're always in compliance, so the SLO provides no signal). Calibrating an SLO requires a quarter of data. Budget for revision. The process of tightening SLOs over time is healthy — it means your reliability is improving.

The "always on time" trap

If you're consistently at 99.97% with a 99.9% SLO, you're burning too much engineering time on reliability. Your users only need 99.9% — you're giving them 99.97% and paying the cost. Either raise your SLO to 99.95% or explicitly redirect that reliability engineering effort toward features. The surplus reliability is waste if users can't perceive the difference.

Budget resets create perverse incentives

Calendar-month budgets reset on the 1st. Engineers learn this quickly. A team that burns through budget on January 3rd knows they have 28 days before the next reset — and may consciously or unconsciously deprioritise reliability work knowing the clock will restart. Rolling windows eliminate this by making the budget always "right now, what's our reliability in the last 28 days?" There's no reset to coast toward.

SLOs don't capture user satisfaction

A service can meet every SLO and still have unhappy users. If your SLO is 99% success rate and users find the 1% failures catastrophically bad (lost orders, corrupted data, failed payments), hitting 99% isn't enough. SLOs measure what you defined — not what matters to users. Supplement SLOs with user-reported feedback and support ticket volume to catch what the metrics miss.

Decision sequence

Implementing SLOs for a service

Work through this in order. Skipping steps is how you get SLOs that nobody uses.

1
Identify the user-facing behaviours that matter
Ask: what can a user do with this service? For each capability, what does success look like from their perspective? A payment API: "the charge completes without error." A search endpoint: "results return within 1 second." These user-facing outcomes become your SLI candidates. Avoid infrastructure metrics at this stage — they're means, not ends.
start from user outcomes, not system metrics
2
Choose SLIs for success rate and latency
For most services: successful_requests / total_requests over a rolling window (success rate SLI), and histogram_quantile(0.95, ...) for the latency SLI. Define "successful" precisely — does a 200 with an error body count? Does a timeout that retries and succeeds count? Write the definition down. You'll need it when incidents happen and people argue about whether a request "really" failed.
write down the exact definition of 'successful request'
3
Look at 90 days of historical data before setting the SLO
What has your service actually achieved? Find the P5 of your historical daily success rate — the worst 5% of days. Set your initial SLO slightly below this. You want a target that's achievable with current infrastructure and represents meaningful reliability, not a number you set aspirationally and then ignore because you're always in breach.
initial SLO = slightly below historical P5
4
Build the dashboard before setting alerts
The dashboard — current SLI value, error budget remaining as a percentage and as time, burn rate over last 1h/6h/24h — needs to be visible to the whole team before you alert on it. If the first time anyone sees the budget number is when an alert fires at 2am, the SLO process has failed. The dashboard is the shared language. Build it first.
dashboard first, alerts second
5
Set burn rate alerts at two windows
Fast burn: alert when 1-hour AND 5-minute burn rate both exceed 14.4× (exhausts budget in 48 hours). Slow burn: alert when 6-hour AND 30-minute burn rate both exceed 6× (exhausts budget in 5 days). Requiring both windows to fire reduces noise. The fast alert pages on-call; the slow alert creates a ticket for next business day.
two-window alerts kill noise
6
Define what the team does at each budget level
Write it down before an incident, not during one. Budget >50%: normal velocity. 10-50%: conservative deploys, no experiments. <10%: freeze features, focus on reliability. 0%: formal freeze, brief leadership, post-mortem. These policies need to be agreed between engineering and product before the first SLO breach. The agreement is what makes the error budget meaningful.
policies agreed in advance, not negotiated during incidents

Section 11

SLO implementation checklist

SLI
User-facing metric

Measures what users experience directly. Success rate + latency at minimum. Written definition of 'successful request.'

SLO
Calibrated target

Set from historical data, not aspirationally. Rolling 28-day window. Different SLOs per service based on criticality.

BDG
Budget visible

Error budget on team dashboard, visible to everyone, updated continuously. Not just checked at retrospectives.

BRN
Burn rate alerts

Two-window alerts: fast burn (1h+5m, 14.4×) pages on-call. Slow burn (6h+30m, 6×) creates ticket.

POL
Policies agreed

What happens at 50%, 10%, 0% budget — agreed between product and engineering before the first breach.

REV
Quarterly review

SLOs reviewed and tightened each quarter as reliability improves. SLOs that never change stop being meaningful.

The airline doesn't aim for zero delays. They aim for enough on-time flights that passengers keep choosing them. The rest of the budget goes into everything else that makes the airline worth flying.

The next time someone says your service needs to be more reliable — ask: more reliable than what, exactly?
