Week 4 · Day 24 · Microservices & Boundaries

The question is never
"microservices or monolith".
It's where to draw the lines.

Every system is a collection of responsibilities. The only real decision is whether those responsibilities live in one process or many — and if many, exactly where one ends and the next begins. Draw the lines in the wrong place and you get a distributed monolith: all the complexity of microservices with none of the benefits. Draw them right and each service can be deployed, scaled, and replaced independently.

Section 01

The restaurant kitchen — and why stations exist

A busy restaurant kitchen is not one chef doing everything. It has stations: grill, sauté, pastry, cold prep. Each station owns its own tools, its own ingredients, its own techniques. The grill chef does not walk into the pastry section and use their oven. The pastry chef does not touch the grill. Each station is a self-contained unit of expertise.

They communicate through the pass. When the grill station completes a steak, it goes to the pass — the counter between the kitchen and the service area. The head chef calls the ticket, coordinates timing, and assembles the plate. No station needs to know how another station works. The grill chef does not know the pastry chef's recipe for the dessert. They only know: the dessert will arrive at the pass correctly when called. That is the contract.

Each station fails independently. If the pastry section runs out of cream and cannot serve desserts tonight, the rest of the kitchen keeps running. Tables still get their starters and mains. The failure is contained. In a kitchen where one chef does everything, a dropped dessert stops the whole service.

Each station scales independently. On a busy Saturday, you add a second grill chef for the grill station. You do not add a second pastry chef — the dessert demand does not require it. You scale the bottleneck, not the whole kitchen.

A new station can be added without reorganising the whole kitchen. The restaurant decides to add a wood-fired pizza station. It gets its own corner, its own oven, its own ingredients. It communicates through the pass like everyone else. No existing station changes what it does.

That is microservices. Each service is a station — owning its own data, its own logic, its own deployment. The API is the pass. The contract is what one station promises to deliver, not how it delivers it. Independence at the station level is what lets the whole kitchen keep running when one part breaks.

🍳
Where the kitchen analogy breaks

A real kitchen has a head chef who sees the whole plate and coordinates timing in real time. In microservices, that coordination is the hardest problem — distributed transactions, saga patterns, eventual consistency. No single "head chef" sees the whole state. Each service sees only its own data and what other services tell it through their APIs. The kitchen analogy explains boundaries well. It undersells the coordination cost of crossing them.

Section 02

How do you know where one service ends and the next begins?

This is the question that microservices architecture mostly fails to answer well. "Make them small" is not an answer. "One service per team" is closer but still not a principle. The correct answer comes from Domain-Driven Design: a service boundary should match a bounded context — a part of the business domain that has its own language, its own rules, and its own data model.

Bounded contexts — the DDD way

In a ticket booking system, the word "customer" means something different to the billing team (a payer with a credit card), the marketing team (a segment profile with preferences), and the support team (a person with a history of complaints). These are different models of the same entity. If you build one Customer model shared across all three, it becomes a mess of fields that nobody fully understands. If you separate them into bounded contexts, each team owns its own clean model.

The practical test: if two teams argue constantly about what a field in a shared model means, that model crosses a bounded context boundary. Split it. Each team gets their own model. They translate between models at the boundary — a process DDD calls the anti-corruption layer.

The strangler fig — migrating without a rewrite

The strangler fig pattern is how you extract services from a monolith without stopping the world. You put a proxy in front of the monolith. New functionality is built as services. Existing functionality is gradually moved out — one bounded context at a time — and the proxy routes traffic to the new service instead. Eventually the monolith is empty and removed. The service grows around the monolith like a fig tree around a host.

Signs your boundaries are wrong

  • Chatty services: Service A makes 8 calls to Service B to complete one operation. The data belongs together. The boundary is in the wrong place.
  • Shotgun changes: Every new feature requires changes to 5 services simultaneously. The boundary cuts across a logical unit of change.
  • Shared database: Two services read and write the same tables. There is no real boundary — just a distributed monolith with extra network hops.
  • Versioning coupling: Services must be deployed in a specific order. They are not actually independent.

Section 03

Data ownership — the rule that makes microservices real

The single most important rule in microservices architecture: each service owns its data, and no other service touches it directly. A service's database is an implementation detail. Other services do not get to query it, join against it, or write to it. They ask the service for data through its API. The service decides what to expose and how.

This rule sounds simple. It is consistently the hardest one to enforce. The temptation to "just join the tables" is overwhelming when you need data from two services and the databases happen to be on the same host. Every shortcut here creates coupling that eventually forces services to be deployed together, upgraded together, and understood together — destroying the independence that made microservices worthwhile.

What does a service own?

A service owns the data that represents the truth about its domain. The booking service owns booking records — it is the authoritative source for whether a booking exists and what state it is in. The payment service owns payment records — it knows whether a payment was authorised and for how much. The booking service does not own payment data. When it needs to know if a booking has been paid, it asks the payment service.

The read model problem

Respecting data ownership creates a practical problem: complex queries. A dashboard that shows "bookings with their payment status and the user's name" needs data from three services. You cannot join three databases. The solution: read models (also called materialised views or query services). A separate service subscribes to events from all three domains, builds a denormalised view optimised for that specific query, and serves it. The booking dashboard service owns nothing original — it just assembles a read view from event streams.

🔴
Shared database is not a boundary — it is a distributed monolith

The most common microservices mistake: splitting services into separate processes but keeping them on a shared database. Service A and Service B both write to the bookings table. They cannot be deployed independently — a schema change breaks both. They cannot be scaled independently — the database is the bottleneck. They cannot be understood independently — changes to the schema affect both. You have paid all the costs of microservices (network latency, distributed transactions, operational complexity) and received none of the benefits (independent deployment, isolation, scalability). The service boundary is the database boundary, not the code boundary.

Section 04

Service contracts — the promise between stations

A service contract is the explicit agreement between a service and its consumers: what the API accepts, what it returns, what errors it can produce, and what guarantees it makes. Without an explicit contract, services drift — the producer changes a field name, the consumer breaks, nobody knows why until 3am.

Contract-first design

Design the API before writing implementation. Define the request and response shapes, the error codes, the versioning strategy, the SLO. Consumers can build against a mock server immediately. Producer teams know exactly what they are building toward. Misunderstandings surface in design reviews, not in production.

Backward compatibility — the non-negotiable rule

Once a service is in production with consumers, every change to its contract must be backward compatible. Adding a field is safe — consumers that do not know about it ignore it. Removing a field breaks consumers that depend on it. Renaming a field is equivalent to removing the old name and adding a new one — breaking. The versioning strategy handles this: /v1/bookings stays stable, new incompatible changes go to /v2/bookings, and v1 is maintained until all consumers have migrated.

Consumer-driven contract testing

The producer cannot know what every consumer depends on. Consumer-driven contract testing (Pact) inverts this: each consumer writes a contract test describing what it expects from the producer. The producer runs all consumer contracts as part of its test suite. If a producer change breaks any consumer's contract, the test fails before deployment. Consumers define the contract, producers verify they meet it.

Section 05

How should services talk to each other?

Two services need to exchange data. They have three options: synchronous HTTP/gRPC (caller waits for response), asynchronous events via Kafka (caller publishes and moves on), or choreography via shared events (services react to what happened without anyone directing them). The choice determines coupling, latency, resilience, and complexity.

Synchronous: use it when the caller needs the answer now

The checkout service needs to reserve a seat before confirming the booking. It cannot move on without the answer — reserved or not. This is a synchronous call: checkout calls inventory, waits for a response, then decides what to do. The tradeoff: the checkout service is now coupled to inventory's availability. If inventory is slow, checkout is slow. If inventory is down, checkout fails (Day 18's circuit breaker applies here).

Asynchronous events: use it when the caller does not need the answer now

After a booking is confirmed, the email service needs to send a confirmation, the analytics service needs to record the event, and the partner webhook service needs to notify external systems. None of these need to complete before the API responds to the user. The checkout service publishes a booking.confirmed event (Day 23) and is done. Each downstream service handles it in its own time, at its own pace, with its own retry logic.

Choreography vs orchestration

Choreography: Services react to events without a central coordinator. Booking confirmed event fires. Email service reacts. Analytics service reacts. No service tells another what to do — they all just watch the event stream and act on what they see. Highly decoupled, hard to trace when things go wrong.

Orchestration: A workflow service explicitly calls each step in sequence. After booking, the orchestrator calls email service, waits for success, calls analytics, waits for success. Clear trace of what happened and in what order. More coupling — the orchestrator must know about every downstream service.

The saga pattern — distributed transactions without distributed transactions

When a business operation spans multiple services — book seat, charge payment, send confirmation — you cannot use a database transaction. The data lives in separate databases. Instead, you use a saga: a sequence of local transactions, each publishing an event that triggers the next step. If any step fails, compensating transactions undo the previous steps. The booking saga: reserve seat (inventory), charge card (payment), confirm booking (booking service). If payment fails, the compensating transaction releases the reserved seat.

Section 06

Start with a monolith. Extract when you feel the pain.

The single most consistent piece of advice from engineers who have built both: do not start with microservices. A monolith that works is infinitely better than microservices that do not. The reasons are numerous but they reduce to one: you do not know your bounded contexts until you have built the system. The pain of wrong boundaries in a monolith is refactoring. The pain of wrong boundaries in microservices is a distributed systems problem requiring team coordination, data migration, and potentially months of work to fix.

The signals that a monolith is ready to be split:

  • Deploy bottleneck: All teams must coordinate on every deployment. A single team's changes block everyone else.
  • Scale mismatch: One part of the system (e.g. the search service) needs 10x the resources of everything else, but you can only scale the whole monolith.
  • Team autonomy blocked: Teams routinely conflict over shared code and cannot ship independently.
  • Technology lock-in: A specific component would genuinely benefit from a different language, database, or runtime.

If you do not feel these pains, the monolith is fine. Adding microservices to solve problems you do not have is one of the most expensive engineering mistakes you can make.

✅
The modular monolith — the best of both

A modular monolith has strict internal boundaries — modules that own their own data models and expose clean interfaces — but deploys as a single process. The BookMyShow booking system at scale started as a modular monolith with well-defined internal modules. When specific components needed independent scaling, they were extracted as services — already cleanly separated because the monolith enforced the boundary. The extraction cost one sprint per service. Building microservices from scratch in year one would have taken six months and produced the wrong boundaries anyway.

Section 07

The failure modes nobody warns you about

The distributed monolith

Services that share a database, deploy together, and cannot be understood independently. The worst of both worlds: you pay the operational complexity of microservices (network latency, service discovery, distributed tracing) while retaining the coupling of a monolith (shared data model, coordinated deployments). The cause: drawing boundaries at the code level without drawing them at the data level.

Nanoservices

Services so small they do one database operation each. A get-user-by-id service, a update-user-email service, a delete-user service. Each is a thin wrapper over a database call, introducing a network hop, a service to deploy, a container to manage, and a contract to maintain — for no benefit. The granularity is wrong. These belong in the same service.

Synchronous chains

Service A calls B, B calls C, C calls D. The checkout request travels through four services synchronously. The latency adds up: 40ms + 30ms + 50ms + 20ms = 140ms before the user gets a response. Worse: if D is slow, A is slow. If C is down, B fails, A fails. The chain amplifies failures. The fix: collapse the chain (maybe A, B, C belong in the same service) or break it with async events.

Premature decomposition

Splitting a system into microservices before understanding the domain. You draw boundaries based on database tables or API endpoints rather than business capabilities. The boundaries are wrong. Changing them later requires data migration across service databases, API versioning across teams, and coordinated deployments. The cost of wrong microservice boundaries is an order of magnitude higher than the cost of wrong module boundaries in a monolith.

Section 08

Production reality

Service mesh — infrastructure for service-to-service communication

At scale, service-to-service communication needs observability, retries, circuit breaking, mutual TLS, and load balancing — applied consistently across every service pair. A service mesh (Istio, Linkerd) handles this at the infrastructure level, via sidecar proxies, rather than requiring each service to implement it. The tradeoff: significant operational complexity. A service mesh is not justified until you have tens of services with serious reliability and observability requirements.

Service discovery — how services find each other

In Kubernetes, service discovery is built in: each service gets a DNS name (payment-service.production.svc.cluster.local) that resolves to the current pod IPs. The load balancer is the kube-proxy. Outside Kubernetes, tools like Consul handle service registration and health checking. The practical rule: if you are on Kubernetes, use its built-in service discovery. If you are not, adopt it before building a service mesh.

Observability across services — the distributed tracing requirement

A request that touches six services produces logs and metrics in six different places. Without a trace ID propagated through every service call, correlating those logs is impossible. Day 19's distributed tracing is a prerequisite for microservices in production. Every service call must propagate the trace context in headers. Every service must emit spans. Without this, debugging a cross-service failure takes hours instead of minutes.

Section 09

The things nobody tells you

Microservices move complexity from code to operations

A monolith has complex code. Microservices have simpler code in each service and dramatically more complex operations: service discovery, distributed tracing, network policies, container orchestration, multi-service deployments, distributed transactions. The total complexity does not decrease — it shifts. Teams that choose microservices for "simplicity" are often surprised by the operational burden. Make the shift intentionally, with eyes open to what you are trading.

Conway's Law is not optional

Conway's Law: your system architecture will mirror your team communication structure. If you have four teams, you will end up with four services — regardless of whether those boundaries are right. This is not a design principle to follow; it is a gravity field to account for. Design your team boundaries and your service boundaries together. If the team structure is wrong, the service structure will be wrong.

The two-pizza rule is a team size heuristic, not a service size heuristic

Amazon's two-pizza rule (a team should be small enough to be fed by two pizzas) is about team size, not service granularity. It means the team that owns a service should be small enough to communicate without formal processes. It does not mean a service should be small enough to fit in two pizzas. A two-pizza team might own a service that handles half of Amazon's checkout flow. Size the team for ownership; size the service for domain boundaries.

Eventual consistency is a product decision, not an engineering decision

Asynchronous microservices produce eventual consistency: after a booking is confirmed, the analytics dashboard may show the old count for a few seconds. Whether that is acceptable is a product question, not a technical one. Engineers often implement eventual consistency without telling product that it means data may be stale. Product often makes requirements that implicitly require strong consistency without knowing it. Align on this explicitly, per feature, before building.

Decision sequence

Should this be a separate service?

Answer these in order before extracting anything from the monolith.

1
Are you actually feeling the pain that microservices solve?
Deploy bottleneck? Scale mismatch? Team autonomy blocked? Technology lock-in forcing bad decisions? If none of these apply, stay in the monolith. Adding microservices to a monolith that works is pure cost with no benefit. The pain you are avoiding is theoretical. The complexity you are adding is real.
no pain = stay in the monolith
2
Can you draw the data boundary first?
Before separating code, identify what data the new service would own exclusively. If the answer is "it shares the same tables as the main service," stop — you are creating a distributed monolith. The service boundary is the database boundary. If you cannot identify clean data ownership, the boundary is in the wrong place.
no data boundary = no real service boundary
3
Does this capability change for independent reasons?
The core question in bounded context identification: does this capability change when other things change, or does it change for its own reasons? Payment logic changes when payment regulations change. Inventory logic changes when venue seating changes. These are different reasons — good candidate for separation. If they always change together, they probably belong together.
same change reason = same service
4
Does the team structure support independent ownership?
A service with no clear owner is a service that will be neglected. Before extracting a service, identify which team owns it, what their on-call rotation looks like, and who reviews its contracts and SLOs. If the answer is "everyone and no one," the service will drift. Conway applies: team boundaries and service boundaries must align.
no owner = service becomes a liability
5
Design the contract before writing the service
Define the API (request shape, response shape, error codes, SLO) before writing a line of implementation. Share it with consumers. Find the mismatches in design reviews, not in production. Use OpenAPI for REST or protobuf for gRPC. Register the schema. The contract is the permanent artifact — implementations can be rewritten, contracts cannot be broken.
contract first = mismatches caught early
6
Use the strangler fig — do not rewrite, extract
Put a proxy in front of the monolith. Route new functionality to the new service. Gradually migrate existing functionality — one endpoint at a time, with traffic comparison to verify correctness. Do not stop serving traffic from the monolith until the new service is verified in production. A big-bang migration is how you break things. A strangler fig is how you do it safely.
strangler fig = zero-downtime extraction

Section 11

Microservices readiness checklist

OWN
Data ownership

Each service has its own database. No other service reads or writes its tables directly. Enforced, not just documented.

CTR
Contract defined

API contract specified (OpenAPI or protobuf) before implementation. Consumer-driven contract tests in CI. Breaking changes blocked.

TRC
Distributed tracing

Trace IDs propagated across all service calls. Every service emits spans. Cross-service request traceable end-to-end.

OWR
Clear owner

Each service has a named owning team with on-call responsibility. No "shared" services without explicit joint ownership.

IDP
Independent deploy

Service can be deployed without coordinating with other services. If it cannot, it is not really a service yet.

SLO
Service SLO

Each service has a defined SLO. Consumers know the availability and latency guarantee. Error budget tracked per service.

The grill station does not care how the pastry section makes its desserts. It only cares that the dessert arrives at the pass when called. That separation — owning your own domain, communicating only through defined interfaces — is what makes the whole kitchen run even when one station has a bad night.

The next time you are deciding whether to split a service — ask: can each part fail, scale, and deploy without the other knowing? If not, the lines are not drawn yet.
