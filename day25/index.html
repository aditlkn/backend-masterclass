Week 4 · Day 25 · Auth: JWT, OAuth2, RBAC

Authentication proves
who you are. Authorisation
decides what you can do.

Most engineers conflate these. A JWT proves you are usr_456. RBAC decides whether usr_456 can access /admin/refunds. OAuth2 lets a third party act on your behalf without ever seeing your password. Getting any of these wrong does not produce a bug — it produces a breach.

Section 01

The hotel key card — and why it cannot be cancelled instantly

You arrive at a hotel and check in at the front desk. The receptionist verifies your passport, checks your booking, and hands you a key card. From this moment, you do not need to return to the front desk every time you want to enter your room. You tap the card against the door reader. The reader checks the card. The door opens. The front desk is never involved again.

That is a JWT. The check-in process is authentication — you proved who you are with a passport. The key card is the token — a physical object that encodes your identity and permissions without requiring the front desk to be consulted. The door reader is the resource server — it verifies the card against a known format and lets you through if it is valid. Your room number and checkout date are embedded in the card itself. The reader does not call the front desk. It trusts the card.

Different floors require different cards. Your key card opens your room on floor 3. It does not open the executive lounge on floor 7. It does not open the gym if you did not pay for the fitness package. The card encodes your access level, and each door reader enforces the right level for that door. That is role-based access control — RBAC. Your card says what you are allowed to do, and each protected resource checks whether your card authorises entry.

The concierge gets you gym access without giving the gym your room key. You want to book a personal training session. The trainer needs to know your name and that you are a guest — but not your room number or checkout date. So the concierge issues a limited pass: "this person is a verified guest and may access the gym between 06:00 and 22:00." The trainer scans the pass. They know what they need to know. Nothing more. That is OAuth2 — a protocol for issuing limited, scoped credentials to a third party on your behalf, without sharing your primary credentials.

Here is the problem that makes hotel security teams nervous: you cannot instantly cancel a key card. If a guest loses their card at 14:00, the receptionist issues a new one. But the old card still works until midnight — the checkout time encoded in the card itself. The door readers do not call the front desk to check if a card has been revoked. They are stateless. They just check the card format and expiry. This is the fundamental tension in JWT authentication: the token is self-contained and fast to verify, but it cannot be cancelled without either adding state (a revocation list that every door reader must consult) or waiting for it to expire. Short expiry times — 15 minutes — are the hotel equivalent of cards that auto-cancel quickly.

🏨
Where the hotel analogy breaks

A physical key card cannot be forged by a guest who knows the magnetic stripe format. A JWT can be tampered with — if the verification algorithm is misconfigured. The signature on a JWT is what makes it unforgeable, and it only holds if you verify it correctly. A door reader that accepts any card without checking the magnetic stripe is not a door reader — it is an open door. Setting algorithms: ['none'] in your JWT library is exactly this. The hotel analogy is perfect for the conceptual model. The implementation details require more care.

Section 02

What is actually inside a JWT?

A JWT is three Base64URL-encoded JSON objects joined by dots: header.payload.signature. The header says which algorithm signed it. The payload carries claims — assertions about the user. The signature proves the payload has not been tampered with since it was issued.

The header

Typically just two fields: alg (the signing algorithm — always specify RS256 or ES256, never HS256 in a microservices context) and typ (JWT). The algorithm field is what attackers target — the alg:none attack tells the server to accept any token without a signature.

The payload — registered and custom claims

Registered claims are standardised field names: sub (subject — the user ID), iss (issuer — who signed this token), aud (audience — who this token is intended for), exp (expiry Unix timestamp), iat (issued at). Custom claims carry your application data: roles, plan tier, organisation ID. Keep the payload small — it is sent on every request.

The signature — what makes it trustworthy

The signature is sign(base64(header) + "." + base64(payload), privateKey). The resource server verifies it with the corresponding public key. If the payload has been altered in any way, the signature does not match and the token is rejected. The signature is what converts a readable JSON object into an unforgeable credential — as long as the private key is kept secret and the algorithm is not none.

🔴
Never use HS256 across services — use RS256 or ES256

HS256 uses a symmetric key: the same secret signs and verifies. Every service that needs to verify a token must have the secret — which means every service can also forge tokens. RS256/ES256 use asymmetric keys: only the auth server has the private key (and signs tokens), services have the public key (and verify tokens). A compromised downstream service cannot forge new tokens, only use the ones it already has. In a microservices architecture, this distinction is the difference between a breach affecting one service and a breach affecting all of them.

Section 03

OAuth2 and OIDC — delegated authorisation

OAuth2 is not an authentication protocol. It is an authorisation protocol — a way for a user to grant a third-party application limited access to their data on another service, without sharing their credentials. The "Login with Google" button is not Google authenticating you — it is Google issuing your application a token that says "this user has granted you access to their email address and profile."

OpenID Connect (OIDC) adds identity on top of OAuth2. OAuth2 gives you access tokens (what can this app do on the user's behalf). OIDC adds ID tokens (who is this user). When you see "Sign in with Google," the identity part is OIDC. The access part (allow this app to read your Gmail) is OAuth2.

The four grant types — which to use

  • Authorization Code + PKCE: The correct flow for web and mobile apps. User logs in on the auth server, gets a code, exchanges code for token. PKCE prevents code interception attacks. Use this for all user-facing applications.
  • Client Credentials: Service-to-service authentication — no user involved. Service A presents its client ID and secret to get a token to call Service B. Use this for backend-to-backend calls.
  • Implicit: Deprecated. Returned tokens directly in the URL. Never use this.
  • Resource Owner Password: Deprecated. App collects username and password and exchanges them directly. Defeats the purpose of OAuth2. Never use this.

PKCE — what it solves and why mobile apps need it

In the authorization code flow, the auth server returns a code to the app's redirect URI. In a mobile app, multiple apps can register the same redirect URI — a malicious app can intercept the code. PKCE (Proof Key for Code Exchange) prevents this: the app generates a random code_verifier, hashes it into a code_challenge, sends the challenge with the initial request, and sends the verifier when exchanging the code. Only the app that started the flow knows the verifier. An intercepted code is useless.

Section 04

RBAC — who can do what

Role-Based Access Control assigns permissions to roles, and roles to users. A user with role admin can do everything a user with role staff can do, plus more. A user with role viewer can read but not write. The mapping is: user has roles, roles have permissions, permissions gate actions on resources.

Where to enforce it

RBAC enforcement should happen at two levels. First in the API gateway or middleware — check that the token includes a role that is allowed to call this endpoint at all. Second in the service logic — check that the user's role allows this specific action on this specific resource. The first check is coarse-grained and fast. The second check is fine-grained and context-aware.

Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC) — when RBAC is not enough

RBAC says "admins can edit bookings." ABAC says "admins can edit bookings in their own region, created within the last 30 days, that have not been paid." ABAC evaluates attributes of the user, the resource, the environment, and the action. It is more expressive than RBAC and significantly more complex to implement and audit. Start with RBAC. Move to ABAC only when you have a concrete case that RBAC cannot handle cleanly.

RBAC middleware — Express + JWTTYPESCRIPT
import jwt from 'jsonwebtoken';

// Verify JWT and attach user to request
export function authenticate(req, res, next) {
  const token = req.headers.authorization?.split(' ')[1];
  if (!token) return res.status(401).json({ error: 'missing_token' });

  try {
    req.user = jwt.verify(token, publicKey, {
      algorithms: ['RS256'],       // explicit allowlist — never omit
      audience:   'booking-api',   // reject tokens not meant for us
      issuer:     'https://auth.example.com',
    });
    next();
  } catch (err) {
    return res.status(401).json({ error: 'invalid_token', detail: err.message });
  }
}

// Authorise by role
export function requireRole(...roles: string[]) {
  return (req, res, next) => {
    const userRoles: string[] = req.user?.roles || [];
    const allowed = roles.some(r => userRoles.includes(r));
    if (!allowed) return res.status(403).json({ error: 'insufficient_permissions' });
    next();
  };
}

// Usage
app.get('/bookings/:id', authenticate, requireRole('user', 'admin'), getBooking);
app.post('/admin/refund', authenticate, requireRole('admin'), issueRefund);
app.delete('/bookings/:id', authenticate, requireRole('admin'), deleteBooking);

// Fine-grained ownership check inside the handler
async function getBooking(req, res) {
  const booking = await db.findBooking(req.params.id);
  if (!booking) return res.status(404).json({ error: 'not_found' });

  // User can only see their own bookings unless they are admin
  if (booking.userId !== req.user.sub && !req.user.roles.includes('admin')) {
    return res.status(403).json({ error: 'forbidden' });
  }
  res.json(booking);
}

Section 05

Token revocation — the hardest problem in JWT auth

A JWT is valid until it expires. There is no built-in revocation. If a user logs out, changes their password, or is banned — their existing JWT still works until expiry. This is not a JWT flaw; it is an inherent property of stateless tokens. The door readers cannot call the front desk. They just check the card.

Three strategies, each with a real cost

Short expiry (15 minutes) + refresh tokens: The access token expires quickly, limiting the window of abuse. A longer-lived refresh token (days or weeks) is used to get new access tokens silently. Revocation works by refusing to issue a new access token when the refresh token is presented — the old access token expires naturally within 15 minutes. The cost: more complexity, more round-trips, and the refresh token itself needs to be stored and revocable (which reintroduces state).

Token blocklist in Redis: On logout or revocation, store the JWT ID (jti claim) in Redis with a TTL matching the token expiry. On every request, check if the jti is in the blocklist. If yes, reject. The cost: every token verification now requires a Redis lookup — you have converted stateless JWTs into stateful sessions with extra steps. At high throughput, Redis becomes a bottleneck and a single point of failure.

Accept the window: Set a short expiry (15 minutes), do not implement a blocklist, accept that a logged-out user can still use their token for up to 15 minutes. For most applications, this is acceptable. A user who logs out on a public computer has at most 15 minutes of exposure. For high-security applications (banking, healthcare), this window is unacceptable and one of the other strategies is required.

⚠️
Storing JWTs in localStorage is an XSS vulnerability

JWTs stored in localStorage are accessible to any JavaScript running on the page. An XSS attack that injects a script can exfiltrate every token in storage. Store access tokens in memory (a JavaScript variable) and refresh tokens in HttpOnly cookies. HttpOnly cookies are inaccessible to JavaScript — only the browser sends them, automatically, on every request to the right domain. This does not eliminate CSRF risk (solved with SameSite=Strict or CSRF tokens) but closes the XSS-to-token-theft attack path.

Section 06

The attacks that actually happen

alg:none — accepting unsigned tokens

The JWT header specifies the signing algorithm. An attacker crafts a token with "alg":"none" and no signature. If the server does not explicitly specify which algorithms to accept, some JWT libraries will accept this token — no signature verification performed. Fix: always specify algorithms: ['RS256'] explicitly. Never let the token header dictate the algorithm used for verification.

Algorithm confusion — RS256 to HS256

A server configured for RS256 uses its public key to verify signatures. An attacker takes the public key (which is public), uses it as an HMAC secret, and signs a token with HS256. If the server accepts both RS256 and HS256, it verifies the HS256 signature with the public key as the HMAC secret — and it matches. Fix: accept only one algorithm. Never accept both RS256 and HS256 on the same endpoint.

JWT secret too weak

If using HS256 (symmetric), the secret must be at least 256 bits of cryptographically random data. Teams that use human-readable strings ("mysecret", "development") or short keys are vulnerable to offline brute force. An attacker who captures a JWT can attempt millions of HMAC verifications per second against candidate secrets. Fix: use RS256/ES256 (asymmetric) for production. If you must use HS256, use crypto.randomBytes(32).toString('hex') for the secret.

Missing audience validation

A JWT issued by your auth server for Service A should not be accepted by Service B. The aud claim specifies the intended audience. If Service B does not validate the audience, an attacker who obtains a token issued for Service A (perhaps through an XSS attack on Service A) can use it against Service B. Fix: always validate aud and iss in your JWT verification.

Section 07

Sessions vs tokens — which should you use?

DimensionServer-side sessionsJWT tokens
State locationServer (Redis/DB)Client (token itself)
RevocationInstant — delete the sessionHard — wait for expiry or use blocklist
ScalabilitySession store can bottleneckStateless — scales horizontally easily
Cross-domainCookie-based, same-domain easierBearer header, works cross-domain
MicroservicesEvery service needs session store accessEvery service verifies locally with public key
SecuritySession ID is opaque — no payload exposurePayload is readable (not encrypted by default)

Use sessions when you need instant revocation (security-critical apps), when you have a single-domain web app, and when you want the simplicity of server-side state management. Redis-backed sessions are fast and revocable.

Use JWTs when you have microservices that need to verify identity without a shared session store, when you need cross-domain or cross-service authentication, and when you are building APIs consumed by mobile apps or third parties. Accept the revocation limitation or mitigate it with short expiry + refresh tokens.

A hybrid approach works well: short-lived JWTs (15 minutes) for API access, with refresh tokens stored in HttpOnly cookies managed by a central auth service. The auth service is the only component with a session store. All other services verify JWTs locally.

Section 08

Production reality

Use an auth provider — do not build it yourself

Auth0, Cognito, Clerk, Supabase Auth, and Keycloak implement OAuth2, OIDC, MFA, social login, token rotation, and session management correctly. Building your own authentication from scratch is a path to subtle security bugs that will not be discovered until they are exploited. The auth provider is one of the very few components where "do not build it yourself" is nearly universal advice. Use a provider for everything except the authorisation logic — RBAC is application-specific and must live in your services.

Rotate secrets and keys on a schedule

JWT signing keys should be rotated every 90 days. JWKS (JSON Web Key Sets) make this transparent to consumers — the auth server publishes its public keys at a well-known endpoint, services fetch them periodically, and rotation happens without downtime. When rotating: publish the new key alongside the old key, wait for all outstanding tokens signed with the old key to expire, then remove the old key.

Rate limiting on auth endpoints

Login, token exchange, and password reset endpoints are targets for brute force and credential stuffing attacks. Rate limit by IP and by account: maximum 10 login attempts per minute per IP, lockout after 5 failed attempts per account, exponential backoff on repeated failures. These limits should be enforced at the API gateway level, not in the auth service code — they need to be applied before the request reaches your application.

Section 09

The things nobody tells you

The JWT payload is not encrypted — it is encoded

Base64URL encoding is reversible. Anyone who can read the JWT — including the user, including logs, including any proxy between client and server — can read the payload. Never put sensitive information in a JWT payload: no PII, no financial data, no internal system details. The payload should contain only what is necessary for auth decisions: user ID, roles, audience, expiry. If you need to pass sensitive data, encrypt the JWT (JWE) or pass it through a secure channel separately.

Clock skew breaks JWT verification in distributed systems

JWT expiry is a Unix timestamp compared against the server clock. If two servers have clocks 2 minutes apart, a token that expired 1 minute ago on one server is still valid on the other. JWT libraries have a clockTolerance or leeway option — typically 30-60 seconds — to handle normal clock drift. Set it. But ensure your systems are NTP-synchronised regardless.

Logout does not work the way users expect

A user clicks "logout." The client deletes the JWT from storage. The user feels logged out. But if they had the token copied somewhere, or if an attacker stole it, the token is still valid until expiry. "Logout" in a stateless JWT system is client-side fiction unless you implement revocation. Be honest in your product about what logout does and does not guarantee. For regulated industries, server-side revocation is a compliance requirement, not an option.

Decision sequence

Designing auth for a new service

Work through this before writing any auth code.

1
Use an auth provider for authentication
Auth0, Cognito, Clerk, Keycloak. Do not implement OAuth2, token issuance, or session management from scratch. The auth provider handles login, MFA, social login, token rotation, and OIDC. Your service handles only authorisation — who can do what once identity is established.
DIY auth is where most auth breaches start
2
Use RS256 or ES256 — not HS256
In any system with more than one service, asymmetric signing means only the auth server can issue tokens. Services verify with the public key. A compromised service cannot forge tokens. Configure your JWT library with an explicit algorithm allowlist: algorithms: ['RS256']. Fetch the public key from the auth server JWKS endpoint, not from configuration.
HS256 means every service can forge tokens
3
Set access token expiry to 15 minutes
15-minute access tokens + refresh tokens is the standard pattern. Short expiry limits the damage window if a token is stolen. The refresh token (stored in an HttpOnly cookie) silently renews the access token. Set refresh token expiry to the session duration you want (7 days, 30 days). Implement refresh token rotation — each use of a refresh token issues a new refresh token and invalidates the old one.
15 min access + rotation = limited breach window
4
Validate iss, aud, and exp on every token
Verify the issuer matches your auth server. Verify the audience matches this service. Verify the token is not expired. All three. A valid signature is not enough — a token signed by your auth server but issued for a different service should be rejected. These fields exist precisely for this reason.
missing aud check = token reuse across services
5
Implement RBAC at two levels: gateway + handler
Coarse check in middleware: does this role have access to this endpoint at all? Fine check in the handler: does this user have access to this specific resource? The first check catches obvious violations early. The second enforces ownership (user can only access their own data). Both are necessary — middleware alone cannot check resource ownership.
two levels = coarse gate + fine ownership
6
Store tokens in HttpOnly cookies, not localStorage
Refresh tokens in HttpOnly; Secure; SameSite=Strict cookies. Access tokens in memory (JS variable). If XSS occurs, the attacker cannot read HttpOnly cookies. They may be able to make requests (CSRF) — mitigated by SameSite=Strict. localStorage is completely readable by any JS on the page — one XSS vulnerability and every token is stolen.
localStorage + XSS = instant token theft

Section 11

Auth production checklist

ALG
RS256 / ES256

Asymmetric signing. Explicit algorithm allowlist in JWT verify. No HS256 in multi-service systems. JWKS endpoint for key rotation.

EXP
Short expiry

Access tokens: 15 minutes. Refresh tokens: 7-30 days with rotation. Never long-lived access tokens in production.

VAL
iss + aud + exp

All three validated on every token. iss: auth server URL. aud: this service. exp: must be in the future. Non-negotiable.

STR
Token storage

Refresh tokens in HttpOnly cookies. Access tokens in memory. No JWTs in localStorage. SameSite=Strict on auth cookies.

RBK
RBAC enforced

Coarse check in middleware (role allowed for endpoint). Fine check in handler (user owns this resource). Both levels present.

RL
Rate limited

Login, token exchange, password reset all rate-limited at gateway. Per-IP and per-account limits. Lockout after N failures.

The hotel key card proves you checked in. It does not prove who you are right now. The door reader trusts it until checkout time — and if the card is lost, the door still opens for whoever has it. Short expiry is not a workaround. It is the design.

The next time an auth bug appears — ask: is this an authentication problem, an authorisation problem, or a revocation problem? They have different causes and different fixes.
