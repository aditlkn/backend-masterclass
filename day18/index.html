Section 01

How cascade failures happen — the anatomy

A cascade failure starts with a single point of slowness — not even a complete failure. A downstream service starts responding in 3 seconds instead of 50ms. Your application makes a call, waits 3 seconds, gets a response. All seems fine. Then traffic increases. Each request ties up a thread for 3 seconds. Your thread pool has 100 threads. At 33 requests per second, the pool is full. New requests wait for a thread. Your response times spike. Health checks timeout. The load balancer removes you. Your users see errors — not from the slow downstream service, but from yours.

This is the core problem: slow dependencies are more dangerous than fast failures. A dependency that responds in 5ms — even with an error — lets you handle it quickly and move on. A dependency that responds in 3000ms holds your resources hostage for 3 seconds per request.

The three failure modes that cascade

  • Latency spike: Service responds, but slowly. Resources held waiting. Thread pool exhausted. Your service becomes slow, which may cascade to your callers.
  • Connection queue: Service not responding at all. TCP connections queue up. Connection pool fills. New requests rejected. Same thread-pool exhaustion, but faster.
  • Retry storm: Service fails. Callers retry. Each retry compounds the load on an already-struggling service. The retries prevent recovery. Without retry jitter and limits, a service trying to recover gets hammered back down.
🔴
No timeout = guaranteed cascade

Every external call — HTTP, database, cache, message queue — must have a timeout. Without a timeout, a slow dependency will eventually hold threads indefinitely. Even if the dependency is usually fast, a single network partition or slow GC pause on the remote side can cause every thread in your pool to wait forever. Set timeouts that reflect your SLA, not "infinity."

Section 02

The circuit breaker pattern

Named after the electrical analogy: a circuit breaker trips when it detects a fault, interrupting current flow to protect the circuit. In software, a circuit breaker wraps a remote call and monitors its success rate. When failures exceed a threshold, it "opens" — rejecting calls immediately without attempting the remote call. This protects both your system (no wasted threads waiting for a failing service) and the downstream service (no traffic hammering something that's already struggling).

The circuit breaker was popularised by Michael Nygard's "Release It!" and implemented prominently in Netflix's Hystrix library. Hystrix is now deprecated in favour of Resilience4j (Java) and similar libraries in other ecosystems. The pattern is language-agnostic.

Why fast-fail matters

When the circuit is open, calls fail immediately — in microseconds, not seconds. This means your thread pool doesn't fill up waiting. Users get an error (or a fallback response) quickly. The error is visible in your metrics. You can act on it. Contrast with no circuit breaker: threads pile up waiting for a service that will never respond, connection pools fill, and you discover the problem when your own health checks fail.

✅
Circuit breakers are not just for microservices

Any external dependency benefits from a circuit breaker: a third-party payment gateway, your own database (yes — a circuit breaker around DB calls protects your application when the DB is overwhelmed), an external API, a cache server. The pattern applies wherever you make a call that might be slow or fail. Don't reserve it only for service-to-service HTTP calls.

Section 03

The three states — and the transitions between them

A circuit breaker has exactly three states. Understanding the transitions is critical because misconfigured thresholds cause two common failure modes: a circuit that opens too eagerly (throttling legitimate traffic during transient errors) and a circuit that opens too slowly (not protecting against a real outage).

CLOSED
→ failure threshold →
OPEN
→ timeout →
HALF-OPEN

CLOSED — normal operation

Requests pass through to the downstream service. The circuit breaker tracks success and failure counts over a sliding window. When the failure rate exceeds the configured threshold (e.g. 50% of requests in the last 10 seconds fail), the circuit transitions to OPEN.

OPEN — fast-failing

All requests are rejected immediately without attempting the call. The caller receives an error (or fallback) in microseconds. The circuit stays open for a configured timeout (e.g. 30 seconds) to give the downstream service time to recover.

HALF-OPEN — recovery probe

After the timeout, the circuit transitions to HALF-OPEN. A limited number of probe requests are allowed through (e.g. 5 requests). If these succeed, the circuit closes and traffic resumes. If they fail, the circuit reopens for another timeout period.

⚠️
Threshold calibration is where most circuit breakers are misconfigured

Too sensitive (threshold: 10% failure rate, window: 5 requests): circuit opens on a single slow request during a legitimate traffic spike, blocking all traffic unnecessarily. Too loose (threshold: 80% failure rate, window: 1000 requests): circuit doesn't open until 800 consecutive failures — by which time you have a full cascade. Start with: failure rate ≥50%, minimum request count of 20 (to avoid opening on low-traffic noise), window of 10-60 seconds. Tune based on observed false-positive open events.

Section 04

Fallbacks: what to do when the circuit opens

An open circuit tells callers "this service is unavailable." What the caller does next — the fallback — determines whether the user experiences a total failure or a degraded but functional experience. Fallback strategy is a product decision as much as an engineering decision: what can users do without, and what must always work?

The fallback hierarchy

  • Cached response: If you have a recently cached version of the data, return it. A product listing that's 5 minutes stale is almost always better than an error. The circuit breaker and cache work in tandem — cache-aside pattern with a circuit breaker around the database is a natural pairing.
  • Default/empty response: Return a safe default. Recommendations service is down? Return an empty array (UI shows "No recommendations right now"). Feature flags service is down? Return the default flag values (safe defaults). Never crash the whole page because one non-critical service is unavailable.
  • Degraded mode: Return a reduced version of the response. Can't reach the personalisation service? Return the generic product listing instead of the personalised one. User still gets a functional page, just not personalised.
  • Queue for later: For write operations, accept the write into a local queue and process it when the downstream service recovers. Appropriate for non-critical writes (analytics events, email sends) where eventual consistency is acceptable.
  • Fail fast with user messaging: For operations where none of the above apply (payment processing, critical writes), fail immediately with a clear message. "This feature is temporarily unavailable. Please try again in a few minutes." Better than a hanging spinner.

Section 05

Retry with exponential backoff and jitter

Retrying a failed request is sensible — transient failures (network glitch, momentary overload) resolve quickly and a retry succeeds. But naive retry logic — retry immediately, retry at fixed intervals — can make a bad situation much worse.

The retry storm problem

If 1,000 clients all experience a failure at the same moment and all retry immediately, the service goes from handling 1,000 requests to handling 2,000 simultaneously at exactly the moment it's already struggling. The retries prevent recovery. This is a retry storm, and it's why services that recover from an outage often experience a second outage immediately after — all the accumulated retries land at once.

Exponential backoff

Each retry waits longer than the last: 1s, 2s, 4s, 8s, 16s... up to a maximum (e.g. 32s). This gives the downstream service progressively more time to recover. But if all 1,000 clients use the same backoff schedule, they all retry at the same moment on each backoff interval — still a storm, just a slower one.

Jitter

Add randomness to the wait: instead of exactly 4 seconds, wait between 2 and 6 seconds. This spreads retries across time, turning a synchronised storm into a smooth spread. AWS's recommended approach: sleep = min(cap, base * 2^attempt) + random(0, 1000ms). The random component breaks the synchronisation.

Retry with exponential backoff + jitterTYPESCRIPT
interface RetryOptions {
  maxAttempts: number;
  baseDelayMs: number;
  maxDelayMs:  number;
  jitterMs:    number;
  retryOn?:    (err: Error) => boolean;  // which errors to retry
}

async function withRetry<T>(
  fn: () => Promise<T>,
  opts: RetryOptions
): Promise<T> {
  let lastError: Error;

  for (let attempt = 0; attempt < opts.maxAttempts; attempt++) {
    try {
      return await fn();
    } catch (err) {
      lastError = err as Error;

      // Don't retry on non-retryable errors (4xx, validation, etc.)
      if (opts.retryOn && !opts.retryOn(lastError)) throw lastError;
      if (attempt === opts.maxAttempts - 1) break;

      // Exponential backoff: base * 2^attempt, capped at maxDelay
      const exponential = Math.min(opts.maxDelayMs, opts.baseDelayMs * Math.pow(2, attempt));
      // Full jitter: random value between 0 and exponential
      const delay = Math.random() * exponential + Math.random() * opts.jitterMs;

      await new Promise(r => setTimeout(r, delay));
    }
  }
  throw lastError!;
}

// Usage
const result = await withRetry(
  () => paymentService.charge(orderId, amount),
  {
    maxAttempts: 3,
    baseDelayMs: 100,
    maxDelayMs:  5000,
    jitterMs:    500,
    retryOn: (err) => {
      // Only retry on transient errors — not on 400 (bad request) or 402 (payment failed)
      if (err instanceof HttpError && err.status < 500) return false;
      return true;  // retry 5xx, network errors, timeouts
    },
  }
);

Section 06

Timeouts: every call needs one

A timeout is the simplest and most important resilience pattern. It bounds the maximum time any call can take. Without it, a slow dependency can hold your thread indefinitely. With it, the worst case is "I waited N milliseconds and gave up" — a bounded, recoverable state.

Setting timeout values

Timeout values should be derived from your SLA, not guessed. If your API has a 500ms response time SLA, your outgoing calls should collectively take no more than ~400ms (leaving 100ms for your own processing). If you make 3 sequential calls, each gets ~130ms. If the normal P99 latency for a call is 80ms, set the timeout at 200-300ms — 2-3× the normal P99, not 10×.

Too tight a timeout causes false positives: legitimate slow responses are treated as failures. Too loose a timeout defeats the purpose: you've just moved the thread-exhaustion problem from "infinite wait" to "30 second wait." The correct timeout is the maximum response time you're willing to accept given your SLA, not the maximum response time the service could theoretically take.

Cascading timeouts

In a chain of services (A → B → C), each service should have a shorter timeout than its caller. If A gives B 500ms, B must complete — including its call to C — within 500ms. B should give C 300ms (leaving 200ms for B's own work). If B sets its timeout to C at 600ms, it guarantees B will timeout before C does — wasting a retry that could have used the remaining time.

⚠️
Passing deadlines, not timeouts, across service calls

When Service A calls Service B with a 500ms timeout, B should know how much time is left, not just that A has some timeout. If A spends 200ms of internal processing before calling B, B has 300ms left — but if A passes a 500ms timeout to B, B thinks it has 500ms and may accept work it can't complete in time. Pass deadlines (absolute timestamps) across service boundaries, not timeouts (durations). gRPC's deadline propagation does this automatically.

Section 07

Bulkhead: isolate failures by resource pool

Named after the watertight compartments in a ship's hull — if one compartment floods, the others stay dry. The bulkhead pattern applies the same principle to thread pools and connection pools: each downstream dependency gets its own isolated pool. If one pool fills up (because a dependency is slow), only calls to that dependency are affected — other dependencies continue operating normally.

Without bulkheads, all calls share a single thread pool. When Service B is slow, its calls hold threads. When those threads are exhausted, calls to Service C — which is working perfectly fine — are also rejected because there are no threads available. Bulkheads prevent this cross-contamination.

Thread pool vs semaphore bulkhead

Thread pool isolation: Each dependency gets a dedicated thread pool. Calls execute in that pool's threads. The overhead: context switching, thread stack memory, scheduling. The benefit: complete isolation — a slow dependency cannot block any thread in the main pool. Used by Hystrix.

Semaphore isolation: Calls still execute in the caller's thread, but a semaphore limits how many concurrent calls can be in flight. Lower overhead. The downside: a blocking call in the caller's thread still holds that thread — only the count is limited, not the thread allocation.

Bulkhead implementation — semaphore isolationTYPESCRIPT
class Bulkhead {
  private inFlight = 0;

  constructor(
    private maxConcurrent: number,
    private maxQueue: number = 0  // 0 = no queuing, fail immediately
  ) {}

  async execute<T>(fn: () => Promise<T>): Promise<T> {
    if (this.inFlight >= this.maxConcurrent) {
      // Bulkhead full — fail immediately (or queue if maxQueue > 0)
      throw new Error(`Bulkhead full (\${this.inFlight}/\${this.maxConcurrent} in flight)`);
    }
    this.inFlight++;
    try {
      return await fn();
    } finally {
      this.inFlight--;
    }
  }

  get utilization() { return this.inFlight / this.maxConcurrent; }
}

// Separate bulkheads per dependency
const paymentBulkhead     = new Bulkhead(10);   // max 10 concurrent payment calls
const inventoryBulkhead   = new Bulkhead(20);   // max 20 concurrent inventory calls
const notificationBulkhead = new Bulkhead(5);   // max 5 (non-critical, intentionally small)

// Usage: if payment is slow, inventory and notification are unaffected
const [paymentResult, inventoryResult] = await Promise.allSettled([
  paymentBulkhead.execute(() => paymentService.charge(amount)),
  inventoryBulkhead.execute(() => inventoryService.reserve(sku)),
]);

Section 08

Health checks: liveness vs readiness

Health checks are how your infrastructure (load balancers, Kubernetes, service mesh) decides whether to send traffic to an instance. Two distinct concepts are often conflated but serve different purposes.

Liveness: is the process alive?

A liveness check answers "is this process running and not deadlocked?" If the check fails, Kubernetes restarts the pod. A liveness check should be extremely simple — just verify the process is responsive. It should never check external dependencies (database connectivity, downstream services). If it did, a downstream failure would cause Kubernetes to kill healthy pods that just happen to be calling a failing service. Liveness failure = "restart me."

Readiness: am I ready to serve traffic?

A readiness check answers "can I currently handle requests?" If it fails, the load balancer removes the instance from the pool. Readiness can check dependencies — if the database connection pool is exhausted or a critical downstream service is unavailable, the instance should stop receiving traffic. Readiness failure = "stop sending me traffic, but don't restart me."

Startup: am I done initialising?

A startup check (Kubernetes 1.18+) gives a pod time to initialise before liveness checks begin. A slow-starting service that takes 60 seconds to warm up would fail liveness checks (which might run every 10 seconds) and get restarted in a loop. Startup probes prevent this.

Health check endpoints — correct implementationTYPESCRIPT + YAML
// Express health check endpoints

// /healthz — LIVENESS: process is alive
// Never checks dependencies — only responds if the process is running
app.get('/healthz', (req, res) => {
  res.status(200).json({ status: 'ok', timestamp: Date.now() });
});

// /readyz — READINESS: ready to serve traffic
// Checks critical dependencies
app.get('/readyz', async (req, res) => {
  const checks = await Promise.allSettled([
    db.query('SELECT 1'),             // DB connection
    redis.ping(),                      // Cache
    // Do NOT check non-critical services here
  ]);

  const dbOk    = checks[0].status === 'fulfilled';
  const redisOk = checks[1].status === 'fulfilled';
  const ready = dbOk && redisOk;

  res.status(ready ? 200 : 503).json({
    ready,
    checks: { db: dbOk, redis: redisOk },
  });
});

Section 09

Production reality

Start with timeouts — they're the most impactful pattern

If you implement nothing else from this day, implement timeouts on every external call. A circuit breaker without timeouts is almost useless — the circuit opens only after connections time out, by which time threads are already exhausted. Timeouts bound the damage; circuit breakers prevent repeat damage. Do timeouts first.

Circuit breakers need dashboards

A circuit breaker that silently switches to OPEN and serves fallbacks is invisible without monitoring. You'll serve degraded responses for hours before noticing. Instrument every circuit breaker: emit metrics for state changes (CLOSED→OPEN, OPEN→HALF-OPEN, HALF-OPEN→CLOSED), fallback invocations, and rejection counts. Alert on circuit OPEN events — they indicate a failing dependency that needs attention.

Don't retry on 4xx responses

A 400 Bad Request won't succeed on retry — the request is malformed. A 401 won't succeed without a new token. A 422 Unprocessable won't succeed with the same payload. Only retry on 5xx responses (server error), network errors (connection refused, timeout), and specific 429 responses (where you respect the Retry-After header). Retrying 4xx is wasted compute and makes your logs noisy.

The recovery problem: circuit in HALF-OPEN

When the circuit transitions to HALF-OPEN and allows probe requests through, those requests may hit a service that's only partially recovered. If the probe fails, the circuit reopens. If the service is flapping (failing every other request), the circuit may oscillate — never fully closing, never staying open. Implement exponential backoff for the OPEN→HALF-OPEN timeout: first time 30s, second time 60s, third time 120s. This gives a flapping service more time to stabilise.

Section 10

The things nobody tells you

Cascading circuit opens are a pattern, not a bug

If Service A depends on Service B, and Service B's circuit opens, Service A will start seeing failures too — its circuit may open as well. This cascade of circuit openings is correct behavior, not a bug. Each service protecting itself from a failing upstream is exactly what resilience patterns are supposed to do. What you should monitor: how far up the dependency chain the circuit opens propagated. That tells you the blast radius of the original failure.

The circuit breaker state must survive restarts

An in-memory circuit breaker resets when the process restarts. After a deployment, all circuits are CLOSED — no memory of previous failure states. If you deploy in the middle of a downstream service outage, your newly deployed pods will immediately start hammering the failing service before their circuits open. For critical paths, persist circuit state in Redis so it survives pod restarts and deploy cycles.

Fallback responses can mask problems

A circuit breaker with a good fallback is comfortable to ignore. "We're serving cached recommendations instead of live ones — good enough." Weeks later, the circuit is still open, you've been serving stale recommendations for weeks, and nobody noticed because the fallback worked. Set TTLs on how long you'll serve fallbacks: after N minutes of serving a fallback, alert the on-call team even if no user-facing error is visible.

Chaos engineering validates your resilience patterns

You don't know if your circuit breakers actually work until they're triggered in production. Chaos engineering — intentionally introducing failures in a controlled way — validates that your resilience patterns behave as expected. Netflix's Chaos Monkey randomly terminates production instances. More targeted: use fault injection in your load balancer or service mesh to introduce latency or errors on specific routes. Do this in production, not just staging.

Decision sequence

Adding resilience to a service call

Apply in this order — each pattern builds on the previous.

1
Set a timeout first — always, no exceptions
Every external call gets a timeout derived from your SLA. If your API must respond in 500ms, your outbound calls should have timeouts of 200-400ms depending on chain length. An unbound call is a thread held hostage indefinitely. Timeouts are the foundation everything else builds on.
no timeout = infinite thread hold = guaranteed cascade
2
Add retry with exponential backoff + jitter for transient failures
Retry only on 5xx and network errors. Max 3 attempts (original + 2 retries) for most cases. Exponential backoff with full jitter to prevent retry storms. Set a max backoff ceiling (e.g. 5 seconds). Don't retry on 4xx — those will never succeed with the same payload.
retry without jitter = synchronised storm on recovery
3
Wrap with a circuit breaker if the call is high-frequency or critical
For calls that happen on every request (database, auth service, product catalog), a circuit breaker prevents thread-pool exhaustion when that service fails. Configure: failure rate ≥50%, minimum 20 requests before opening, 10-second window, 30-second open timeout. Emit metrics on state transitions.
circuit breaker = fast fail when downstream is struggling
4
Design the fallback before adding the circuit breaker
A circuit breaker without a fallback just converts "slow response" into "fast error." Decide what to return when the circuit opens before writing the circuit breaker code. Cached data? Empty response? Degraded version? For critical paths with no acceptable fallback, skip the circuit breaker and focus on the upstream service's availability.
circuit breaker without fallback = fast error instead of slow error
5
Add bulkheads for non-critical or unreliable dependencies
External third-party services (payment gateway, SMS provider, analytics) should have their own resource pools. If they're slow, they shouldn't affect calls to your own database. Limit concurrent calls per dependency based on expected load: 10 concurrent calls to a payment gateway is usually sufficient; 100 to your primary database may be too few.
bulkhead = failure isolation across dependencies
6
Monitor state transitions and set alerts
A circuit that opens silently is invisible. Emit a metric on every state change. Alert when any circuit has been OPEN for more than 5 minutes — it means a dependency is struggling and needs attention. Track fallback invocation rate — a sustained fallback rate is a signal of systemic degradation, not just transient failures.
unmonitored circuit breakers = false confidence in resilience

Section 12

Resilience checklist

TMO
Timeouts everywhere

Every external call has a timeout derived from your SLA. No unbounded calls. Database, cache, HTTP — all have explicit timeouts.

JTR
Jitter on retries

No fixed-interval retries. Exponential backoff + full jitter. Max 3 attempts. Never retry on 4xx responses.

FLB
Fallback defined

Every circuit breaker has a fallback defined before deployment. Cached data, safe default, or explicit degraded mode.

LVS
Liveness ≠ readiness

/healthz checks only process health. /readyz checks dependencies. Never check downstream services in liveness — it causes restart storms.

BLK
Bulkhead 3rd parties

External services (payment, SMS, analytics) have isolated resource pools. One slow provider can't exhaust threads for everything else.

ALT
Alert on OPEN

Every circuit OPEN event emits a metric. Alert when circuit stays open >5 min. Unmonitored circuit opens = silent degradation.
